When most kids use computers today, they use them as **consumption devices**.

They watch videos.  
They scroll through apps.  
They play games.

What they rarely learn is how computers actually work — or how to **create things with them**.

That realization is why I built this curriculum.

<!-- truncate -->

---

## The Problem I Kept Seeing

When I talked with parents and teachers, a pattern kept appearing.

Kids were growing up surrounded by technology, but they often didn't understand:

- how files are saved  
- how computers respond to inputs  
- how search engines work  
- how to tell if information online is trustworthy  
- how software and digital tools are actually built

Even more surprising: many kids felt **intimidated by computers**, despite using them every day.

They knew how to open apps.

They didn’t know how to **figure things out**.

That gap bothered me.

---

## What I Believe Kids Should Learn Instead

Computer literacy shouldn't just be about learning specific apps.

Software changes constantly.

Instead, kids should learn **mental models** that stay useful for life.

Things like:

- Computers respond to **inputs and instructions**
- Digital work can be **saved, organized, and improved**
- The internet is created by **people**
- Algorithms influence what we see online
- Computers are powerful tools for **creation**, not just entertainment

Those ideas help children develop **confidence and curiosity**, which matter far more than memorizing menus in a specific program.

---

## A Curriculum Built Around Curiosity

This course was designed around a simple philosophy:

> Kids learn best when they explore.

Each week includes short sessions where students:

- experiment with tools
- try things and see what happens
- ask questions
- build small creations
- reflect on what they discovered

Instead of step-by-step instructions, the lessons encourage students to **think like problem solvers**.

---

## What Kids Actually Build

Over the course of the curriculum, students gradually learn to create things with a computer.

They will explore:

- writing and communication
- digital art and images
- internet research and media literacy
- simple coding
- generative AI tools
- planning and building digital projects
- optional 3D design with TinkerCAD

By the end, students complete a **final project they design themselves**.

The goal isn’t perfection.

The goal is ownership.

---

## The Real Goal

The most important outcome of this course is not a specific skill.

It’s a mindset.

By the end of the program, students should feel:

- comfortable exploring technology
- confident trying new tools
- capable of solving simple digital problems
- aware of how the internet works
- proud of something they created

In other words:

> Computers should feel like tools they can understand and control — not mysterious machines.

---

## Why I Made It Public

I originally created this curriculum for teaching and mentoring my own daughter.

But the more I worked on it, the more I realized something:

Many parents want to teach these skills… but **don’t know where to start**.

So I decided to publish the full curriculum online for anyone to use.

If it helps even a few kids discover that computers are tools for **thinking, building, and creating**, then it was worth it.
