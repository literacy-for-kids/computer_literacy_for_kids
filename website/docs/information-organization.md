# Information Organization Skills for Digital Life

Computer literacy includes files, folders, project materials, screenshots, code blocks, bookmarks, notes, and versions. Information Organization helps kids name, sort, and find digital information.

This page is the local doorway into the Literacy for Kids [Information Organization Toolkit](https://www.literacy-for-kids.com/docs/toolkits/information-organization), connected to the digital skills this curriculum builds.

## A few core ideas

- **Filenames are labels for future-you.** "project" today is a mystery next month.
- **Folders are categories.** They are chosen, and they can change.
- **Versions help people track changes.** v1, v2, final — future-you will thank you.
- **Project notes can show what belongs together.** Sprites, sounds, and steps have homes.
- **Digital organization protects work from disappearing.**

## When this shows up

- When saving files
- When organizing folders
- When tracking Scratch or code versions
- When collecting screenshots or research
- When losing a project file
- When keeping AI, chat, or research notes
- When building a final project

## Tools that help

- **Future-you filename test** — will I know what this is later?
- **Folder category check** — does everything have a home?
- **Version label** — name + v-number or date.
- **Project material list** — what pieces exist and where they live.
- **Source/location note** — where information or assets came from.

:::tip Information Organization Moment
A filename is a label for future-you. "project" might make sense today, but "scratch-game-maze-v2" is easier to find later. Good labels help your digital work come back when you need it.
:::

:::caution Everyday organization skills, not a replacement for support
These are everyday information organization tools, not therapy, disability evaluation, academic intervention, legal advice, medical advice, or a replacement for trusted adults, educators, caregivers, accommodations, or qualified support. Kids should not be treated as lazy or broken when information feels messy or hard to organize. If information feels consistently overwhelming, inaccessible, unsafe, private, sensitive, or too big, involve a trusted adult and appropriate support.
:::
