# Collaboration Skills for Digital Projects

Computer projects often involve shared files, paired debugging, Scratch projects, presentations, AI experiments, and final builds. Collaboration helps kids define the project, choose roles, avoid taking over, and leave clear handoffs.

This page is the local doorway into the Literacy for Kids [Collaboration Toolkit](https://www.literacy-for-kids.com/docs/toolkits/collaboration), connected to the digital skills this curriculum builds.

## A few core ideas

- **Digital group work needs visible roles.** The person at the keyboard is not the only person working.
- **Shared files need shared rules.** Naming, saving, and handoffs should be decided together.
- **Debugging together works better with check-ins.** "What is done, what is stuck, what help is needed?"
- **One person should not take over the keyboard every time.** Rotating roles keeps the work shared.

## When this shows up

- When building a Scratch or coding project
- When sharing files or folders
- When debugging together
- When making a presentation
- When using AI or research tools as a group
- When finishing a final digital project

## Tools that help

- **Project goal card** — what are we building, and what does done look like?
- **Driver/navigator roles** — one drives the keyboard, one watches the goal and asks questions.
- **File handoff checklist** — name it, save it, tell the next person where it is.
- **Debugging check-in** — the three check-in questions during a bug hunt.
- **Finish together credit** — who built, tested, designed, and checked?

:::tip Collaboration Moment
In a digital project, the person touching the keyboard is not the only person working. A navigator, tester, question asker, or recorder can also help the team. Roles make invisible work visible.
:::

:::caution Everyday collaboration skills, not unsafe conflict management
These are everyday collaboration tools, not therapy, bullying intervention, conflict mediation, legal advice, or a replacement for trusted adults, educators, caregivers, or qualified support. Kids should not be expected to manage unsafe, coercive, bullying, discriminatory, or threatening group situations alone. If group work feels unsafe, targeted, or too big, involve a trusted adult.
:::
